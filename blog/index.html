Introducing openSUSE Reach

written by Nycticorax on 2020-12-30

openSUSE Reach

"openSUSE Reach" conceptualizes a web application meant to strengthen the openSUSE community along four dimensions

  1. facilitating knowledge transfer across the community
  2. facilitating recruitment of new users, contributors and maintainers
  3. helping openSUSE sub-projects strive
  4. creating stronger community bonds

Rules

  1. A sub-project is defined, specifying the following properties:
    • a start and optionally, an end date
    • a description of the goal and first milestone
    • a specification of the required skills, on the scale <"welcoming greenhorns", "experienced", "veterans">
    • a min. and optionally, a max. number of participants
  2. Whenever a user joins the community they are offered the option to opt out from oS Reach. If they don't opt out, they must fill a form asking for their skills across different preexisting categories, as well as the max. of projects they agree to concurrently work on.
  3. At any point a user can express their interest to a sub-project my subscribing to it. This means they will receive email notifications whenever os Reach thinks they would be a good fit for a position in the project. Whenever a user is sent a notification inviting them to contribute to a project, they can accept it. Accepting an invite materializes a commitment between users and sub-projects.
  4. Whenever oS Reach detects a change in the system, it sees whether it can act on it for the purpose of matching members and projects. oS Reach tries to fill all opened positions across all sub-projects, closing positions whenever a user accepts an invite to fill it, keeping track of all positions and sending invite notifications. In doing so oS Reach prioritizes subscribed users over non-subscribed users, but explores matches further into non-subscribed users territory when subscribed users are not enough.
  5. When all positions in a sub-project are occupied, the project maintainer is notified appropriately. The project maintainer can then agree to close the gates for the project (a project maintainer can close the gates at any time). If the gates are closed, all participants are notified that work is starting, and the project can no longer be subscribed to. From then on the project maintainer is responsible for updating oS Reach on all things regarding the sub-project.

My wishes for the openSUSE community 3

written by Nycticorax on 2020-12-23

This is the third post in a series of three where I list things -- in no particular order -- that I sincerely wish will improve over the upcoming years, starting with 2021, with the openSUSE community. Each wish targets a value I believe is essential for the community to blossom.

This last post is a bit particular, for it's not exactly a new things: it's just me threading together the last two ideas, trying to take advantage of their potential for synergies.

Piecing things together: serious apprenticeship with improved visibility

So openSUSE needs to make it easier for people to discover what other people are working on (that's the improved visibility part), and openSUSE also needs to make it easier for knowledge to flow from seasoned contributors to newcomers (that's the _serious apprenticeship part). Can we eliminate overhead and make one thing out of these two?

I think we can. First let me break the idea into smaller bits, then provide a concrete implementation for the idea within the openSUSE infrastructure, and finally identify some pain points that skeptics are likely to identify, trying to address their worries as we go.

Actually wait, let's jump directly to the implementation. I think it's so simple that it speaks for itself. To makes things vivid I will present it as part of a sequence that goes like this:

Subscription

A new user finds out out openSUSE distributions. Because there are reminded a couple of time on both the website linking to the installer(s) and by the installer itself, they create an openSUSE "OpenID" account on our infrastructure. Well, in fact they do more than just creating an account; they subscribe to potential contribution areas. Concretely, they fill in a form that goes like this:

a. Do you want to participate to the "suggest me something to do / suggest them something to do" programme? _yes_ / _no_
b. Please tick things you would like to do:
[] answer to posts or instant messages soliciting user support: [] check if similar past experience
[] moderating communication channels and platforms: [] check if similar past experience
[] writing documentation: [] check if similar past experience
[] writing memos or posts for internal or external communication: [] check if similar past experience
[] doing web design: [] check if similar past experience
[] contributing to packages; [] check this box if interested to maintain: [] check if past experience 
[] developing and maintaining the infrastructure stack: [] check if similar past experience 
[] engineering, developing and maintaining the core system packages: [] check if similar past experience 
[] organizing community activities and events: [] check if similar past experience 
[] getting involved in the governance or institutional bodies of openSUSE (Board, Election Committee...): [] check if similar past experience
c. Do you want to subscribe to a "waiting queue" so that we will get in touch with you as soon as we find a job or task that resonates with your interests? Your participation to a "waiting queue" will not be published. You will be able opt out at any point. _yes_ / _no_
d. Do you accept to have your contact details published on the website for other people to review it? Your participation to a "waiting queue" will not be published. _yes_ / _no_

Now imagine there is a symmetrical counterpart of this form available to seasoned users, maintainers and generally speaking to anyone in the community looking for contributors to his thing. Using the very same infrastructure, they can express their wish to get a hand, whether for the short or the long term. This means that now offer and demand can be subject to

Matching

Here we don't need machine learning shenanigans. This is not Tinder. And we don't have the numbers anyway. Instead we can keep it simple, stupid and count the number of matching boxes between those looking for contribution opportunities and those looking for contributors. We can also rank matches based on how much time someone looking for a contribution opportunity spent in the waiting queue -- starting with all contribution-seekers who've answered 'Yes' to (c,d), and moving on to those who've subscribed only to (c) if no match was found. We can also make the ranking sensitive to how many past contributions someone has made (i.e. to ensure a fair distribution between contributors).

Records

Summing up: so far we have got people who'd like to contribute and projects they can contribute to visible to the system. Of those who've expressed their desire to contribute, some of them are in waiting queues, and some of them are visible on a public platform. All of these people can be matched at any point, giving priority to those in a waiting queue. And now comes the notion of Records. Let's say I contribute to project A and then move on to B. Based on my experience with A, the system should be able to fall back on me should it fail to match anyone currently looking for a contribution in the system. That is, the matching algorithm should go something like "priority queue & subscribed > subscribed > former contributor". "Former contributors are the "Old Guard", a like in a Napoleon's army, it makes sense to call the Old Guard when the other lines of fire have failed.

But wait a minute! you will say. How do we apply a temporal frame around contributions after a matching has occurred? For we sure need something like an agreement between contribution-seekers and contributors-seekers. Here's a simple way of going about it: when a match occurs, both parties "handshake", i.e. confirm that they agree to play the game. Similar handshakes can re-occur at certain predetermined intervals, say, after 1 weeks, after 3 weeks, after 1 month, etc. The scaling does not matter for present purposes. We now have a workflow where we can keep track of actual contributions, giving visibility to projects, contributors, and maintainers.

Now make all this publicly visible (in a way compatible with both parties' privacy preferences), and you've fixed both the visibility and the knowledge transfer problem affecting the openSUSE community.

My wishes for the openSUSE community: Apprenticeship for motivated newcomers

written by Nycticorax on 2020-12-22

This is the second post in a series of three where I list things -- in no particular order -- that I sincerely wish will improve over the upcoming years, starting with 2021, with the openSUSE community. Each wish targets a value I believe is essential for the community to blossom.

Knowledge transfer: serious apprenticeship

There is no better way to improve one's skills at using something, than do use it. This applies to programming, configuring one's desktop environment, or Linux distribution. And who better than knowledgeable, seasoned users to show us how it's done?

I feel almost bad for saying this, because it seems to be such an obvious fact -- and yet I very often see communities where it is either mysteriously forgotten, or insufficiently taken into account.

This is perhaps the most important suggestion I wanted to make in this blog series. For it can potentially secure so many values. Let's list them to fix ideas:

  • give everyone a sense of belonging here: seasoned Linux users can suffer from "maintainer fatigue" and loneliness after volunteering for several years without much recognition. Or the maybe have settled for an overly rigid or outdated workflow. Giving them soneone to help them and to look after might fix all these problems. Teaching and showing motivated newcomers is a really refreshing experience. As for the newcomers taking the apprenticeship, it creates a sense of responsibility (if only by proxy) and spices up their experience as community members. This fuels motivation to contribute and indirectly helps with recruiting, since newcomers might now extract more value from their interactions with the community.
  • break the status quo: Answering questions and explaining how things are done might question assumptions you haven't challenged for years -- when you even remember making the assumption. And passionate volunteers might come up with genuinely insightful remarks or ideas.
  • identify bottlenecks or loopholes: Withing communities of volunteers we're used to have things work "well enough". But we seldom reach the full potential of what we do. That could be moderating a communication channel, maintaining a package, organizing a survey or simply editing documentation or guidelines. But sometimes you cannot see the whole in the water pipe unless you send it enough pressure. The simple fact that someone with apprentices can tap into a larger pool of skills and energy means that their worflow is mechanically going to intensify, exposing bottlenecks or loopholes. This may indirectly improve the quality of life for everyone in the community.

Now perhaps you were already convinced of how important apprenticeship in Linux communities were. Or perhaps I've managed to convince you, or to strengthen your conviction. So how do we do this at openSUSE?

Between the many things to balance out, three come to mind:

  1. fairness. Everyone who shows motivation and the basic skills to learn should be accepted. There should be no favoritism.
  2. liberty. Participants should be able to choose the area in which they want to volunteer, and responsible people / maintainers should be able to set a ceiling to the number of people they can supervise concurrently. We don't want this to be a burden to our maintainers.
  3. simplicity. At no point should it be a burden to anyone in particular to make the apprenticeship system work. In other words, the burden should be evenly distributed over at least enough people to avoid draining anyone in particular, but at most as many people for it to never become a fragmented process, where no one is responsible of anything or don't know enough to do their job.

The third aspect is the most difficult. I am not aware of the details of how Fedora does it, but I suspect they do have something that compares to the apprenticeship I have in mind. And I suspect it works something like this:

  1. host groups. The community nominates 1 responsible person for every important area: system development, packaging, infrastructure, documentation, communication and marketing, community events / activities. Then each nominee is responsible for build a team around them of decently knowledgeable people. Each team will be responsible for welcoming apprentices and host them.
  2. mentors. After joining a group apprentices are assigned one mentor, who is simply a seasoned Linux user and knows their way around in the community. Of course mentors can be members of host groups. Their job is very simple: do their thing by offloading some tasks to their apprentice(s). They should also answer questions now and then about their workflow and tools, and they should try to work transparently, in the open, so that apprentices can see how things are done.
  3. debriefing and public record. After some times apprenctices can be debriefed. This means that they will asked feedback about their apprenticeship, and will be allowed to keep a public record of their activity, so as to prove their experience. The public record should be discoverable by anyone at anytime, and work as an "phonebook" linked against the former apprentice's profile (or any form of digital personal reference in the community).

I don't want to bother you with more details, but I hope you'll agree that such a system can be implemented so as to meet the (1-3) constraints above reasonably well.

Thanks for reading and see you soon!

My wishes for the openSUSE community: A community-wide pinboard

written by Nycticorax on 2020-12-20

This is the first post in a series where I list things -- in no particular order -- that I sincerely wish will improve over the upcoming years, starting with 2021, with the openSUSE community. Each wish targets a value I believe is essential for the community to blossom.

Transparency: a community wide "pinboard" with a changelog section

Transparency is not just the crucial virtue for organizations. It also an important precondition for efficient communication and coordination. The openSUSE community transparency is severely hampered by the deafening absence of changelog. We have no clue about the infrastructure changes that Heroes have made recently, are making right now, and are preparing for the near future. This creates a situation where we come across unexpected issues. The issues can be broken down into two types:

  • blockers: some piece of the infrastructure is actually unavailable, blocking an entire process, just because someone forgot to say it has always been down ("We should have upgraded since for years but never have"), or because someone forgot to say it was undergoing maintenance, or because some tool supposed to work under condition a, b and c only works under conditions a & b, or because someone somewhere in the hierarchy (or more perspicuously: in the pool of self-assigned responsibilities) decided that this tool was not meant to be used that way.
  • redundancies: you have been working for several hours on an idea, and on a concrete implementation for it. You are enthusiast because you've thought through potential caveats, found a way to overcome them or avoid them or to mitigate the pain, only to find out that someone else, perhaps on the other side of the planet, perhaps the person next door, has come to similar conclusions, and that your project and theirs overlap to a very large extent. You are left to sigh and to blame yourself for not knowing this person in the first place. Except you couldn't know them, nor could have. That's where the pain lies. Had there been a "community pinboard" where they might have described their project, you could have got in touch and sync up.
  • unwarranted privileges: this one might require some elaboration. In a group of people cooperating without a very steep pyramid-shaped structure of power, and where responsibilities are assigned mostly via volunteership and tacit acceptance, there are "excesses" of power that can very well build up in certain crucial quarters. For Linux communities these typically concern infrastructure, simply because the infrastructure cuts across all layers of the tech stack on which the community stands. There are other reasons, for example the fact that infrastructure people usually control not only whether this or that project or initiative can work, but how it can work -- under which conditions --, when it can work and who will be the first let known whether, how, and when it will work. In a world where information about things is inseparable from power to do things, there is a real danger in letting infrastructure people do their things without any requirement to define and publish the course on which they're set. This means at the very least making clear what their goals are, and to report back as soon as the situation has changed, whether or not the goals have been met.

The fix is however straightforward:

  • create a community wide changelog registering all recent changes to the infrastructure and to other important projects;
  • make the changelog a part of a community-wide "ongoing projects pinboard" where anyone can describe what they're trying to do, and quantify the kind of help they might need.
