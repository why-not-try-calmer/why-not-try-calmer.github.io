My wishes for the openSUSE community: A community-wide pinboard

written by Nycticorax on 2020-12-20

This is the first post in a series where I list things -- in no particular order -- that I sincerely wish will improve over the upcoming years, starting with 2021, with the openSUSE community. Each wish targets a value I believe is essential for the community to blossom.

Transparency: a community wide "pinboard" with a changelog section

Transparency is not just the crucial virtue for organizations. It also an important precondition for efficient communication and coordination. The openSUSE community transparency is severely hampered by the deafening absence of changelog. We have no clue about the infrastructure changes that Heroes have made recently, are making right now, and are preparing for the near future. This creates a situation where we come across unexpected issues. The issues can be broken down into two types:

  • blockers: some piece of the infrastructure is actually unavailable, blocking an entire process, just because someone forgot to say it has always been down ("We should have upgraded since for years but never have"), or because someone forgot to say it was undergoing maintenance, or because some tool supposed to work under condition a, b and c only works under conditions a & b, or because someone somewhere in the hierarchy (or more perspicuously: in the pool of self-assigned responsibilities) decided that this tool was not meant to be used that way.
  • redundancies: you have been working for several hours on an idea, and on a concrete implementation for it. You are enthusiast because you've thought through potential caveats, found a way to overcome them or avoid them or to mitigate the pain, only to find out that someone else, perhaps on the other side of the planet, perhaps the person next door, has come to similar conclusions, and that your project and theirs overlap to a very large extent. You are left to sigh and to blame yourself for not knowing this person in the first place. Except you couldn't know them, nor could have. That's where the pain lies. Had there been a "community pinboard" where they might have described their project, you could have got in touch and sync up.
  • unwarranted privileges: this one might require some elaboration. In a group of people cooperating without a very steep pyramid-shaped structure of power, and where responsibilities are assigned mostly via volunteership and tacit acceptance, there are "excesses" of power that can very well build up in certain crucial quarters. For Linux communities these typically concern infrastructure, simply because the infrastructure cuts across all layers of the tech stack on which the community stands. There are other reasons, for example the fact that infrastructure people usually control not only whether this or that project or initiative can work, but how it can work -- under which conditions --, when it can work and who will be the first let known whether, how, and when it will work. In a world where information about things is inseparable from power to do things, there is a real danger in letting infrastructure people do their things without any requirement to define and publish the course on which they're set. This means at the very least making clear what their goals are, and to report back as soon as the situation has changed, whether or not the goals have been met.

The fix is however straightforward:

  • create a community wide changelog registering all recent changes to the infrastructure and to other important projects;
  • make the changelog a part of a community-wide "ongoing projects pinboard" where anyone can describe what they're trying to do, and quantify the kind of help they might need.
